// WHY ANHYDROUS CONDITIONS

RMgX is both a nucleophile and an extremely strong base. If water is present, RMgX deprotonates it immediately, forming an alkane and destroying the reagent before it can attack the carbonyl. Dry ether or THF — aprotic solvents with no acidic protons — are mandatory.

// WATER HYDROLYSIS (WORK-UP)

Once the Grignard reagent has been fully consumed, water is added to protonate the alkoxide (R–O⁻) and give the final alcohol. Always the last step — never add water at the start or during the reaction. Adding water too early destroys the reagent before it can attack the carbonyl.

// ORGANOLITHIUM REAGENTS

Organolithiums (RLi) are even more reactive than Grignard reagents. Must be used at −78°C under inert atmosphere (N₂ or Ar). Common examples: n-butyllithium and phenyllithium. Both aldehydes and ketones react with organolithiums.

// STEP BY STEP MECHANISM

SELECT A STEP TO SEE ITS DIAGRAM

1
RMgX dissociates: the carbanion is born▼
In solution, RMgX partially dissociates. The carbon end picks up both electrons from the C–Mg bond, forming R⁻, a carbanion. Carbon with a full negative charge — unusual and highly reactive. MgX⁺ is the spectator.
The more unusual the charge on an atom, the more desperate it is to react. R⁻ is carbon in an unstable state.
2
R⁻ attacks the carbonyl carbon: new C–C bond forms▼
R⁻ donates its lone pair into the pi* of the carbonyl at carbon. A new C–C bond forms. The pi bond breaks — electrons move onto oxygen forming an alkoxide. Carbon rehybridizes sp² to sp³.
Every time a Grignard reacts, a new C–C bond is born. The most fundamental bond in organic chemistry.
3
Why you cannot add water yet▼
R⁻ is such a strong base that it deprotonates water instantly, forming R–H and destroying the Grignard reagent. The carbonyl never gets a chance to react. The entire reaction must be run in aprotic solvents.
R⁻ does not care whether it deprotonates water or attacks a carbonyl. If water is present, water wins — it's faster.
4
Water hydrolysis: protonating the alkoxide▼
Once the Grignard reagent is fully consumed, water is added. It protonates the alkoxide (R–C–O⁻) to give the final alcohol. Always done last — the Grignard must be completely used up first.
Never add water first. The Grignard eats it. Water only comes in at the end, as a clean-up step to convert the alkoxide to an alcohol.

// WHY MOST GEM-DIOLS ARE UNSTABLE

Gem-diol formation is reversible and the equilibrium usually favours the carbonyl. Most gem-diols cannot be isolated because they immediately revert to the aldehyde or ketone. The C=O bond is more stable than two C–OH bonds in most simple cases.

// CHLORAL HYDRATE — STABILISED BY H-BONDING

Chloral (CCl₃CHO) forms a stable gem-diol. The two adjacent OH groups form intramolecular hydrogen bonds with each other, locking the gem-diol form in place and preventing reversion to the carbonyl. The electron-withdrawing CCl₃ group also helps by making the carbonyl carbon more electrophilic.

// NINHYDRIN — STABILISED BY H-BONDING

Ninhydrin exists as a gem-diol in the solid state. The two OH groups on the central carbon form strong intramolecular hydrogen bonds supported by the adjacent carbonyl groups on the ring. Famous for the ninhydrin test — reacts with amino acids to give a purple colour used in forensic chemistry.

// 1,2-DIHYDROXYCYCLOPROPANE — STABILISED BY RING RESONANCE

In the cyclopropane ring system, the gem-diol is stabilised by the interaction of the OH lone pairs with the strained C–C sigma* orbitals of the ring. This resonance effect unique to three-membered rings donates electron density into adjacent bonds, stabilising the gem-diol over the carbonyl form.

// STEP BY STEP MECHANISM

SELECT A STEP TO SEE ITS DIAGRAM

1
Water oxygen attacks carbonyl carbon▼
The oxygen of water uses one of its lone pairs to attack the carbonyl carbon. The new O–C bond forms, the pi bond breaks, electrons move onto the carbonyl oxygen. An oxyanion intermediate forms. Carbon goes sp² to sp³.
Water is a weak nucleophile — oxygen is already partially satisfied. But the delta+ on carbon is attractive enough to pull it in.
2
Proton transfer gives the gem-diol▼
The oxyanion picks up a proton from solvent water, giving the gem-diol — two OH groups on the same carbon. From the Latin geminus, meaning twin. In most cases the equilibrium lies back toward the carbonyl, so the gem-diol is a transient species.
3
When the gem-diol is stabilised: chloral, ninhydrin, cyclopropane▼
In chloral and ninhydrin, the gem-diol is stabilised by intramolecular hydrogen bonding between the two adjacent OH groups — they lock each other in place, preventing dehydration back to the carbonyl. In 1,2-dihydroxycyclopropane, stabilisation comes from OH lone pairs interacting with the strained sigma* orbitals of the ring — a resonance effect unique to three-membered rings.
H-bonding is the gem-diol's seatbelt. Once the two OH groups are holding each other, they don't let go easily.

// WHY ACID IS NEEDED

Dry HCl provides H⁺ which protonates the carbonyl oxygen, converting it from a moderate electrophile into a highly activated one. The protonated carbonyl now carries a full positive charge on oxygen, making it far more reactive toward the weak alcohol nucleophile.

// HEMIACETAL VS ACETAL

The first alcohol addition gives a hemiacetal — one OH and one OR group on the same carbon. Under acidic conditions, the OH gets protonated and leaves as water, forming an oxocarbenium ion. A second alcohol attacks to give the acetal — two OR groups, no OH.

// ACETALS AS PROTECTING GROUPS

Acetals are stable to basic conditions and nucleophiles. Convert the carbonyl to an acetal, do your chemistry elsewhere in the molecule, then remove the acetal under acidic aqueous conditions to regenerate the carbonyl.

// DRIVING THE EQUILIBRIUM

Acetal formation is reversible. Water is a product. Continuously removing water via Dean-Stark trap or drying agent forces equilibrium right by Le Chatelier's principle.

// STEP BY STEP MECHANISM

SELECT A STEP TO SEE ITS DIAGRAM

1
Acid protonates carbonyl oxygen: activating the electrophile▼
The carbonyl oxygen donates a lone pair to H⁺, protonating itself. This converts the delta+ on carbon into a full positive charge — the carbonyl is now a much stronger electrophile. Without this activation, the weak alcohol nucleophile cannot attack fast enough.
2
Alcohol attacks: hemiacetal forms▼
The oxygen lone pair of the alcohol attacks the activated carbonyl carbon. A new C–O bond forms. After proton loss, the hemiacetal is obtained — one OR group from the alcohol, one OH from the former carbonyl oxygen. Carbon is now sp³.
The hemiacetal is the halfway house. One alcohol in, but there is still an OH group that can leave under acidic conditions.
3
Acid protonates hemiacetal OH: water leaves▼
The OH of the hemiacetal gets protonated, converting it into OH₂⁺ — an excellent leaving group. Water departs, forming an oxocarbenium ion, stabilised by the adjacent oxygen lone pair.
OH is a terrible leaving group. OH₂⁺ is a great one. Protonation converts a bad leaving group into a good one.
4
Second alcohol attacks: acetal forms▼
A second alcohol attacks the oxocarbenium ion. After proton loss, the acetal is the product — two OR groups, no OH. No OH means base cannot deprotonate it and initiate reversal. This is why acetals are stable to basic conditions.
The acetal is the locked form. No OH means no easy way back. You need acid and water together to break it open again.

// WHY SULFUR ATTACKS, NOT OXYGEN

Sulfur is larger and more polarisable than oxygen. Its electron cloud is more easily distorted, making it a softer and better nucleophile for soft electrophiles like the carbonyl carbon. This is an application of HSAB theory

// HSAB THEORY

Hard and Soft Acids and Bases (Pearson, 1963). Hard acids prefer hard bases; soft acids prefer soft bases. Hard = small, high charge density, not very polarisable. Soft = large, low charge density, highly polarisable. The carbonyl carbon is a relatively soft acid. Sulfur is a soft base. Oxygen is harder. So sulfur wins.. Oxygen in HSO₃⁻ is harder and less nucleophilic toward carbon.

// STERIC LIMITATION

Bisulfite addition only works with aldehydes, methyl ketones, and cyclic ketones up to 8 ring members. The bisulfite ion is bulky. Ketones with large alkyl groups create too much steric hindrance during the sp² to sp³ geometry change.

// CRYSTALLINE PRODUCT AND PURIFICATION

The bisulfite adduct is an ionic solid that precipitates as a crystalline product. Convert an aldehyde to its adduct, filter off the crystals, wash away impurities, then regenerate the pure aldehyde by treating with acid or base.

// REVERSIBILITY

The adduct is decomposed by both acid and base. With acid, the sulfonate is protonated and leaves. With base, the alkoxide is deprotonated and sulfonate is expelled as SO₂ and water. Either way the pure aldehyde is regenerated.

// STEP BY STEP MECHANISM

SELECT A STEP TO SEE ITS DIAGRAM

1
Sulfur lone pair attacks carbonyl carbon▼
The sulfur atom of HSO₃⁻ donates its lone pair into the pi* of the carbonyl. Sulfur attacks rather than oxygen because it is larger and more polarisable. New C–S bond forms, pi breaks, alkoxide forms.
Bigger atoms have more loosely held electrons. Sulfur's electron cloud stretches toward the electrophile more easily than oxygen's.
2
Proton transfer: S–H protonates the alkoxide▼
The S–H proton transfers to the alkoxide oxygen in an intramolecular proton transfer, giving the final bisulfite adduct. Highly ionic, precipitates from aqueous solution as a crystalline solid.
The proton is already sitting on the sulfur, right next to where it needs to go. Intramolecular proton transfers are fast.
3
Reversal: acid or base breaks the adduct▼
With base: OH is deprotonated, alkoxide expels sulfonate as SO₃²⁻, regenerating the carbonyl. With acid: sulfonate is protonated and leaves as H₂SO₃ which becomes SO₂ and H₂O. Either way, the pure aldehyde is regenerated.
You use this reaction like a handle. Grab the aldehyde with bisulfite, pull it away from impurities, let go with acid or base to get it back clean.
